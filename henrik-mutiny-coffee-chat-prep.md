## Research Findings on Henrik

### Career Arc

Henrik Berggren is a Swedish-born, NYC-based product and design leader with a distinctive career that blends founder DNA with operator scale. His trajectory tells you a lot about what he values:

This is where Henrik learned to operate at scale inside a large, design-forward company.

This is deeply personal. He went from Big Tech back to zero-to-one, solving a problem he lived with every day. Acquired by Carbon Health.

4. **Head of Product & GM at Carbon Health (2021-2022)** -- Led integration post-acquisition, rebuilt the diabetes monitoring program, and ran cross-program growth.

5. **VP Product & Design at Big Health (2022-2024)** -- Led clinical-grade mental health products (Sleepio, Daylight). Initiated AI transformation. Rebuilt the flagship product on a modern platform. Expanded portfolio through acquisition.

6. **Head of Product & Design at Mutiny (May 2025-present)** -- Leads the full product and design org for an AI-powered B2B GTM platform. Colleagues note he's brought "awesome energy" to the R&D organization.

## About Mutiny: The Product and the Challenge

### What Mutiny Does

Mutiny is an AI-powered GTM (go-to-market) platform that unifies sales and marketing to help B2B companies close enterprise deals faster. Core capabilities:

- **Website Personalization**: Dynamically customize website messaging, images, and CTAs based on visitor firmographics, behavior, and funnel stage. No code required.
- **1:1 Microsites**: AI-generated, fully personalized landing pages for each target account. Instead of sending prospects to a generic page, sales reps send a microsite tailored to the prospect's industry, pain points, and buying stage.
- **Account Intelligence (Account Studio)**: Centralized workspace to segment target accounts, build dynamic lists, and enrich company data with AI. Scans news, earnings calls, etc. to surface talking points.
- **ABM Campaigns**: Draft creative with AI, build personalized campaigns, and push directly to channels like LinkedIn.
- **Campaign Builder & Creative Agent**: The core surfaces the Senior Product Designer role would own -- where marketers and sellers generate, edit, and launch personalized campaigns across accounts.

### The Design Challenge

This is not a simple SaaS dashboard. The core tension at Mutiny is:

1. **AI automation vs. human control**: The Creative Agent generates content, but marketers need to trust it, edit it, and maintain brand consistency. How do you design for "speed and trust" simultaneously?
2. **1:1 personalization at scale**: The platform needs to handle both individual, bespoke microsites AND broad 1:many campaigns -- without the UX feeling schizophrenic.
3. **Enterprise complexity, startup simplicity**: Customers include Uber, Qualtrics, Amplitude, and LaunchDarkly. Enterprise needs (ABM, compliance, approval workflows) must coexist with the "launch in 15 minutes" promise.
4. **Multiple user types**: Marketers (strategic, brand-conscious), sellers/SDRs (pragmatic, speed-focused), and leadership (ROI-focused). One product, very different mental models.
5. **AI-native interaction patterns**: Chat interfaces, visual editors, approval workflows, and AI-generated creative all need to feel like one coherent experience.

### Backed By

Sequoia Capital, Tiger Global, Insight Partners, Y Combinator. CMOs of Snowflake, Airbnb, Carta, and AngelList are advisors/backers.

### Company Values

- **Work should feel like play**: "We do our best, most creative work when we feel safe, energized, and completely immersed." Bring your full self, quirks and all.
- **Faster always wins**: Speed is a competitive advantage. Ship, learn, iterate.
- **Stir the pot, regularly**: Challenge assumptions. Do not be comfortable.
- **Do the right thing when no one's watching**: Integrity and ownership.
- **All hands on deck**: No "that's not my job." Everyone contributes.
- **Live in the world you want to change**: Use your own product. Understand the customer's reality.

### Engineering/Design Culture

- Weekly EPD (Engineering, Product, Design) meetings to evaluate tradeoffs
- Engineers meet directly with customers
- "Own it end-to-end" -- from inception to customer success
- Pair programming and peer code reviews
- Pragmatic prioritization -- interview customers to find the most valuable aspects
- "High pace of execution and technical excellence are not at odds"

## What I'm Really Looking For (Henrik's POV)

If I'm Henrik, here is what is actually running through my mind in this 30-minute coffee chat:

### The Core Question I'm Trying to Answer

**"Can this person own the design of an AI-native creative tool for enterprise users -- and do it with the craft, speed, and product thinking that our stage demands?"**

I'm not looking for someone who needs to be told what to design. I've run Product and Design together my entire career. I want a designer who thinks like a product person and ships like a founder.

### The Signals I'm Scanning For

1. **Product intuition, not just design skill.** Can they talk about WHY they made design decisions, not just WHAT they designed? Do they understand the business problem underneath the pixels?

2. **Comfort with AI-native interaction design.** This is not traditional SaaS. We are designing experiences where an AI agent generates content and the human reviews, edits, and approves. If they have never thought deeply about human-AI collaboration patterns, that is a problem.

3. **Craft sensibility.** I co-founded Readmill because I believed reading could be beautiful. I care about the details. I want to see that they sweat the micro-interactions, the typography, the polish -- not just the wireframes.

4. **Speed and pragmatism.** "Faster always wins" is a company value. I need someone who can ship a strong 80% solution on Monday, not a perfect 100% solution in three weeks. Can they talk about times they made hard scoping tradeoffs?

5. **Enterprise empathy without enterprise bloat.** Our customers are Uber, Qualtrics, Amplitude. These are sophisticated buyers with real workflows. I do not want a designer who dismisses enterprise complexity or who drowns in it. I want someone who can make complex things feel simple.

6. **End-to-end ownership.** At Mutiny, designers are expected to go from user research to shipped product. They meet with customers. They sit in on sales calls. I am looking for someone who is hungry for context, not someone who waits for a brief.

7. **Collaboration style.** We do weekly EPD meetings. I need someone who can hold their own with strong engineers and PMs, advocate for design quality without being precious, and move fast in a small-team environment.

8. **Genuine curiosity about our problem space.** Have they thought about what makes B2B personalization hard? Have they used Mutiny or a competitor? Do they have an opinion about where AI-powered marketing tools are headed?

## Questions I'd Likely Ask and What Good Answers Look Like

### Opening / Warmup

**"Tell me about yourself and what drew you to Mutiny."**

- **Good answer**: Specific, shows they have done their homework. References a particular aspect of the product (the Campaign Builder, microsites, the AI agent approach). Connects it to something in their own experience or interests. Shows genuine excitement about the AI-native design challenge.
- **Red flag**: Generic "I love the mission" or "I want to work on AI" without specifics. No evidence they have looked at the product.

### Design Craft and Process

**"Walk me through a project where you had to design for a complex workflow. How did you make it feel simple?"**

- **Good answer**: Starts with the user problem, not the UI solution. Describes the complexity they had to absorb and the specific design decisions that reduced cognitive load. Talks about tradeoffs they made and why. Shows the before/after in terms of user impact, not just aesthetics.
- **What I really want to hear**: Evidence that they can take a multi-step, multi-stakeholder enterprise workflow and create an interface that feels intuitive on first use. Bonus if they talk about progressive disclosure, smart defaults, or contextual guidance.

**"Show me something you designed that you think is really well-crafted. What makes it good?"**

- **Good answer**: They pick something specific, zoom into the details, and explain WHY each choice was made. They talk about typography, spacing, interaction patterns, and how those choices serve the user's goal. They have a point of view on quality.
- **Red flag**: They show a pretty mockup but cannot explain the reasoning. Or they only talk about high-level strategy and never get into the actual design decisions.

### AI-Specific Design Thinking

**"How do you think about designing experiences where AI is generating content? What are the hardest design problems there?"**

- **Good answer**: Talks about trust, transparency, and human control. References the tension between AI speed and human judgment. Discusses patterns like preview/edit, confidence indicators, undo/revert, side-by-side comparison. Mentions the risk of "uncanny valley" content or loss of brand voice. Ideally references specific products they have used or studied (Figma AI, Notion AI, ChatGPT, Jasper, etc.) and what works and what does not.
- **What I really want to hear**: "The hardest part is not generating the content -- it is designing the right level of human oversight without killing the speed advantage of AI." Or: "The key design problem is trust calibration -- helping users understand when the AI is confident vs. when they should review closely."

**"If you were designing Mutiny's Creative Agent from scratch, how would you approach it?"**

- **Good answer**: Asks clarifying questions first (who is the primary user? what is the current flow? what are the biggest complaints?). Then sketches a framework: starts with the user's goal, thinks about the conversation between human and AI, considers how to balance automation with control. Acknowledges they do not know everything and describes how they would learn quickly.
- **Red flag**: Jumps straight to a solution without understanding the problem. Designs something that sounds like ChatGPT-in-a-box without considering the specific needs of B2B marketers.

### Product Thinking

**"Tell me about a time you disagreed with a PM or engineer on a design direction. What happened?"**

- **Good answer**: Describes the disagreement with respect, explains their reasoning, shows they listened to the other perspective, and describes the resolution. Ideally, they changed their mind on something or found a third option. Shows they can be a strong design advocate without being adversarial.
- **What I really want to hear**: Evidence that they operate as an equal partner with product and engineering, not as someone who "takes orders" or someone who "fights for design."

**"What's a product you admire that does personalization or AI-generated content well? What would you change about it?"**

- **Good answer**: Has a specific, thoughtful answer. Can articulate what the product gets right and where it falls short. Shows they are a student of the space, not just focused on their own work.
- **Red flag**: Cannot name a single product in this space, or gives a surface-level answer.

### Speed and Pragmatism

**"How do you decide when a design is 'good enough' to ship?"**

- **Good answer**: Talks about identifying the core user value and optimizing for that. Describes how they distinguish between "polish that matters" (core interaction, critical first impressions) and "polish that can wait" (edge cases, secondary flows). References real examples of shipping fast and iterating.
- **What I really want to hear**: "I think about the cost of delay. If we are learning something important from users by shipping, the imperfect version is more valuable than the perfect version that ships two weeks later."

### Culture and Motivation

**"What does 'work should feel like play' mean to you in practice?"**

- **Good answer**: Shows they bring genuine energy and curiosity to their work. Describes an environment or moment where they were in flow. Does not interpret "play" as "unserious" -- connects it to creativity, experimentation, and joy in the craft.
- **Red flag**: Awkward silence or a forced answer. This value is central to Mutiny's culture, and if it does not resonate, it might not be the right fit.

## What Would Make Me Champion This Candidate

If I'm Henrik, I am going back to Jaleh (CEO) and the team and saying "we need to hire this person" if:

1. **They showed me they think in systems, not screens.** They talked about the Campaign Builder not as a set of pages but as a system of interactions between a human, an AI agent, and an end-customer. They thought about the flows, the states, the edge cases, and the feedback loops.

2. **They had a specific, informed opinion about Mutiny's product.** They signed up for a demo, or explored the marketing site deeply, or read the microsites blog post, and came in with observations: "I noticed X about how you handle Y -- I was curious about the thinking behind that." This shows initiative and product sense.

3. **They demonstrated founder-level ownership.** Maybe they talked about a time they went way beyond "design" -- running user interviews, writing product specs, building prototypes in code, sitting in on sales calls. This is what Mutiny's culture demands.

4. **They nailed the AI design conversation.** They did not just talk about "AI is cool" -- they had a nuanced view on the design challenges of agentic AI. They referenced the trust spectrum, the control/automation tradeoff, and how to design guardrails that do not feel like speed bumps.

5. **They showed taste and craft in the details.** In their portfolio walkthrough or in how they described past work, they zoomed into the small decisions -- the microcopy, the transition timing, the information hierarchy -- that reveal a designer who sweats the details.

6. **They asked me questions that showed they understand what makes this role hard.** Something like: "How do you think about the tension between letting the AI do its thing and giving marketers enough control to feel confident?" or "What's the biggest gap between what the AI can generate today and what your enterprise customers actually need?"

7. **They felt like someone I'd want to design-jam with.** This is a coffee chat. Henrik co-founded a design-forward company, builds side projects, and leads Product AND Design. He wants a creative collaborator, not a pixel-pusher. If the conversation felt like two product-minded designers geeking out about hard problems, that is the best possible outcome.

## Red Flags I Watch For

1. **"Just tell me what to design."** Any signal of passivity or waiting for specs is disqualifying at this stage and company.

2. **Portfolio-as-performance.** Beautiful Dribbble shots with no explanation of the problem being solved, the constraints, or the impact. Henrik cares about craft, but craft in service of outcomes.

3. **No curiosity about the product or space.** If they have not looked at Mutiny's website, tried the product, or thought about B2B personalization at all, that signals they are applying broadly rather than intentionally.

4. **Discomfort with ambiguity.** Mutiny is building a new product category. If the candidate needs a fully scoped brief to do good work, they will struggle here.

5. **Design preciousness.** Designers who fight to the death over every pixel and cannot ship until it is perfect will clash with "faster always wins." Henrik wants high standards AND high velocity.

6. **No opinion on AI-native design.** This is the central design challenge of the role. If they have not formed any perspective on how to design for AI-generated content, they are behind.

7. **Talking only about themselves.** The best designers are curious about the team, the product, the users. If the conversation is all "I, I, I" with no questions or engagement with Mutiny's reality, that is a miss.

8. **Enterprise snobbery or enterprise fear.** Either dismissing B2B as "boring" or being intimidated by enterprise complexity is a bad sign. The best answer is finding the elegance inside the complexity.

## Pro Tips for the Candidate

### Before the Conversation

1. **Explore Mutiny's product deeply.** Go to mutinyhq.com/product. Read the microsites blog post. Look at their ABM campaigns page. Watch any demo videos. Sign up for a demo if possible. You want to walk in with at least one specific observation or question about the product.

2. **Study Henrik specifically.** He co-founded a design-obsessed reading app, founded a health startup from personal experience, and builds AI tools on the side. He is not a typical "Head of Product" -- he is a design-minded founder who happens to lead product. Speak his language: craft, user empathy, building from zero, AI intuition.

3. **Have a prepared perspective on AI-native creative tools.** Think about: What does a good AI content generation interface look like? What are the failure modes? How do you build trust? Reference specific products (Jasper, Writer, Notion AI, Figma AI, Adobe Firefly, Mutiny's competitors like Tofu, Intellimize). Have an opinion.

4. **Pick your best "complex workflow made simple" case study.** Henrik's job listing explicitly calls for "transforming complex enterprise needs into intuitive, simple experiences." Have a crisp story ready that demonstrates this exact skill.

### During the Conversation

5. **Lead with genuine curiosity, not credentials.** Henrik is a founder-type. He will respect someone who is genuinely fascinated by the problem over someone who recites their resume. Ask thoughtful questions early: "I've been thinking about the challenge of designing an AI agent that generates marketing content at scale -- how do you think about the trust layer for enterprise customers?"

6. **Talk about the intersection of product and design.** Henrik leads both functions. Show that you think about business impact, user behavior, and metrics -- not just visual design. Say things like "the design decision that mattered most was X because it drove Y behavior."

7. **Demonstrate your thinking in real-time.** If Henrik asks how you would approach a design problem, do not give a rehearsed answer. Think out loud. Ask clarifying questions. Sketch the problem space verbally. Show your design brain at work. This is what working together would feel like.

8. **Reference specific details from your research.** Mention Readmill's design philosophy if it connects to your values. Reference a Mutiny value like "faster always wins" naturally when discussing shipping speed. Note that you saw the role involves the Campaign Builder and Creative Agent and that you have been thinking about what those experiences should feel like.

9. **Show you understand the B2B buyer.** Mutiny's end users are marketers and sellers at companies like Uber and Qualtrics. These people are sophisticated, time-pressed, and skeptical. Show that you understand their world -- they do not want to babysit an AI tool, but they also cannot afford to let it go rogue with their brand.

10. **Ask a question that signals depth.** Some strong options:
    - "How do you think about the design system when you have fundamentally different interaction patterns -- chat-based AI, visual editing, and campaign management -- that all need to feel like one product?"
    - "What's been the hardest UX problem to solve in the Creative Agent so far?"
    - "How does the team balance personalization quality at the individual account level with the need to scale across hundreds or thousands of accounts?"
    - "You've built products from zero-to-one multiple times -- what's different about the design challenges at Mutiny compared to your earlier companies?"
    - "How do you think about the role of design in building trust with enterprise buyers who are skeptical of AI-generated content?"

### Tone and Energy

11. **Match Mutiny's "work should feel like play" energy.** Be energized but genuine. Be opinionated but curious. Be confident but not arrogant. Henrik's career is defined by passion projects (Readmill, Steady Health) and intellectual curiosity (side projects, angel investing). He wants to feel like this candidate is someone who would make the work more fun and more interesting.

12. **Be direct and concise.** This is a 30-minute coffee chat, not a 60-minute panel interview. Do not ramble. Have tight, punchy answers with specific examples. Leave room for genuine dialogue.

13. **End with forward momentum.** Express specific excitement about the Campaign Builder / Creative Agent design challenge. Say something like: "Honestly, the design problem you're solving with the Creative Agent -- figuring out how to make AI-generated personalization trustworthy at enterprise scale -- is exactly the kind of problem I want to spend the next few years on. I have a lot of ideas and I'd love to go deeper."
